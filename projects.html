Online Database Index Selection Using Metrical Task Systems

Date: July 2024 - October 2024 | Institution: University of Melbourne

This research project formed my Master's thesis at the University of Melbourne, supervised by Dr. William Umboh.

I took the Advanced Algorithms and Data Structures (COMP90077) subject during Semester 1 of 2024, where I was introduced to some very interesting online algorithms, including one for the set cover problem (utilizing linear programming). William was one of the subject coordinators, and he introduced me to the idea of Metrical Task Systems (MTS) and had been contemplating using it to develop an algorithm for database index selection. This ultimately culminated in my thesis, where I expanded on William's idea and developed a unique MTS-based approach for database index selection (i.e. an MTS algorithm which autonomously makes decisions for materializing and dropping secondary indexes in a database, based on workload patterns). I then demonstrated empirically that my prototype performed exceptionally well when benchmarked against a current state-of-the-art Multi-Armed Bandit-based algorithm.

The project combined elements from a diverse set of topics including: combinatorial optimization, online algorithms, machine learning and AI, recommender systems, and database tuning. It was an incredibly rewarding experience that not only brought together the knowledge and skills I had acquired from my coursework but also challenged me to push the boundaries of my abilities and explore new ideas.

Automated Fact Checking of Climate Claims

Date: Apr 2024 - May 2024 | Institution: University of Melbourne

This research project was completed for the Natural Language Processing subject (COMP90042) at Uni Melbourne, during Semester 1 of 2024.

The project tackles the problem of automated fact verification, where a climate claim statement is presented to an algorithm which must then perform two major tasks: (1) classify the statement into one of 5 different categories of claim veracity (i.e. the degree of authenticity of the statement). and (2.) it must retreive evidence paragraphs from a pre-defined knowledge source to support its classification decision. I developed a 2-stage tranformer based machine learning approach to solve the problem, with the first stage consisting of a BM-25 retrieval module augmented with transformer based re-ranking, followed by a transformer-based classifier. I also utilized a multi-task pretraining strategy to further enhance the model's performance.

Prior to taking the COMP90042 subject, I had become interested in NLP and acquired significant background knowledge through self-study. This project gave me the opportunity to thoroughly study a braod range of topics including: internals of the transformer architecture, information retreival systems, automated question-answering (which is a similar to the claim verification task) and transfer learning. I also gained facility with using the PyTorch deep-learning framework and in the art of systems design.
